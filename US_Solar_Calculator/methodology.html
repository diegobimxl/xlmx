Methodology and Scientific Sources

US Solar Calculator — Technical Document v1.0 — March 2026

1. Solar Irradiance Model

1.1 Peak Sun Hours (PSH)

Solar irradiance is expressed as Peak Sun Hours (PSH), equivalent to kWh/m2/day. One PSH represents 1 hour of irradiance at 1,000 W/m2 (Standard Test Conditions, STC). Values used are annual averages per city derived from the following sources:

Primary source: NASA POWER (Prediction of Worldwide Energy Resources), Surface Solar Radiation (SSR) database, 0.5° x 0.5° resolution, 1991-2020 climatological average. Parameter: ALLSKY_SFC_SW_DWN (all-sky surface shortwave downward irradiance).

URL: https://power.larc.nasa.gov/

Secondary source: NREL National Solar Radiation Database (NSRDB), TMY3 data (Typical Meteorological Year), 4km x 4km resolution from GOES satellite imagery combined with ground station validation. Parameter: GHI (Global Horizontal Irradiance).

URL: https://nsrdb.nrel.gov/

Tertiary source: Global Solar Atlas 2.0 (World Bank / Solargis), satellite-based model at 250m resolution, validated against ground stations. Used for cross-validation.

URL: https://globalsolaratlas.info/

1.2 Tilt and Orientation Factor

The model applies a combined tilt and orientation (azimuth) factor on Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) to estimate Plane of Array (POA) irradiance.

POA = GHI x Tilt_Factor x Orientation_Factor

Tilt factor: simplified latitude-optimal model. For latitude L of each city, optimal tilt is approximately L degrees facing south. At 0° (flat), factor = 1.0. At optimal tilt, factor = 1.15 (15% gain). Beyond optimal tilt, factor decreases linearly.

If pitch <= L: TiltFactor = 1.0 + 0.15 x (pitch / L)
If pitch > L: TiltFactor = 1.15 - 0.15 x ((pitch - L) / (45 - L))

Orientation factors based on azimuth loss studies for mid-latitude northern hemisphere locations (25-48°N):

OrientationFactorNote
South (S)1.00Optimal for northern hemisphere
SSE / SSW0.97Minimal loss
SE / SW0.92Moderate loss
East / West0.82Morning or afternoon irradiance only
NE / NW0.60Minimal direct irradiance
North (N)0.50Opposite to sun — worst case

Reference: Duffie, J.A. & Beckman, W.A. (2013). Solar Engineering of Thermal Processes, 4th Ed. Wiley. Chapters 1-2.

Reference: Marion, B. et al. (2014). "Performance parameters for grid-connected PV systems." NREL/CP-520-37358.

For north-facing orientations (N, NE, NW), tilt reduces output instead of increasing it, applying a 2% penalty per degree of tilt:

TiltFactor(North) = 1.0 - 0.02 x pitch

2.1 Annual Generation Calculation

Annual_Generation (kWh) = System_kWp x Effective_PSH x 365 x System_Efficiency

Where Effective_PSH = Base_PSH x Tilt_Factor x Orientation_Factor

2.2 System Sizing

Daily_Consumption (kWh) = (Monthly_Bill / Rate_per_kWh) x 12 / 365
Required_kWp = Daily_Consumption / (Effective_PSH x Efficiency)
Num_Panels = min( ceil(kWp x 1000 / Panel_Wp), floor(Roof_sqft / Panel_sqft) )

The system is capped at the lesser of: panels needed to cover consumption, or panels that physically fit on the roof.

3.2 Installation Cost

Reference cost: $2.75 USD per Watt-peak installed. This reflects the post-Federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) cost for residential systems in 2025. Includes: panels, microinverter/string inverter, racking, wiring, permitting, labor, and utility interconnection.

Source: EnergySage Solar Marketplace — 2025 installation cost data across 50 states. https://www.energysage.com/

Source: NREL — U.S. Solar Photovoltaic System and Energy Storage Cost Benchmarks Q1 2025. https://www.nrel.gov/solar/market-research-analysis.html

Note: The Federal ITC (Investment Tax Credit) at 30% under the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) is NOT included in this calculator's ROI. Including ITC would reduce effective cost to ~$1.93/W and improve ROI by approximately 30%. This model is conservative.

3.3 ROI Calculation

Total_Investment = System_kWp x 1000 x Cost_per_W
Annual_Savings = Annual_Generation x Rate_per_kWh
ROI (years) = Total_Investment / Annual_Savings
Breakeven (months) = Total_Investment / Monthly_Savings
25yr_Net_Profit = (Annual_Savings x 25) - Total_Investment

5. Model Limitations

This simplified model has the following limitations:

1. Flat rate assumption: assumes constant electricity rate over 25 years. In reality, US residential rates have increased 2-4% annually. Actual ROI is faster than calculated.

2. No annual degradation: does not model ~0.5%/year panel degradation (except in 25yr CO2 calculation where a 0.95 factor is applied).

3. No seasonal variation: uses annual average PSH. Actual production varies by month (higher in summer, lower in winter).

4. No net metering specifics: assumes 1:1 net metering credit. Some utilities (notably California NEM 3.0) use time-of-use export rates that significantly reduce the value of exported solar.

5. No Federal ITC: the 30% Investment Tax Credit under the IRA is NOT included. Including it would reduce payback by ~30%.

6. No state/local incentives: does not include SRECs (Solar Renewable Energy Credits), state tax credits, or utility rebates that vary significantly by location.

7. Simplified tilt factor: uses linear model instead of Perez or Liu-Jordan models for diffuse radiation.

8. No temperature derating: does not model the negative temperature coefficient (-0.35%/°C above STC 25°C), relevant in hot climates like Phoenix and Las Vegas.

9. National average CO2 factor: uses US national average rather than regional eGRID subregion factors, which vary from ~0.2 (hydro-heavy Pacific NW) to ~0.9 (coal-heavy Midwest).
